1. Find the 20th derivative of the function \( f(x) = \frac{1}{3x - 4} \).

Rewrite the function:

\[ f(x) = (3x-4)^{-1} \]

First derivative:

\[ f'(x) = -1 \times 3 \times (3x-4)^{-2} \]

Second derivative:

\[ f''(x) = -1 \times 3 \times -2 \times 3 \times (3x-4)^{-3} \]

Pattern:

\[ f^{(n)}(x) = (-1)^n \times n! \times 3^n \times (3x-4)^{-n-1} \]

Thus, 20th derivative:

\[ f^{(20)}(x) = (-1)^{20} \times 20! \times 3^{20} \times (3x-4)^{-21} \]

Final Answer: Positive (since \((-1)^{20} = 1\))

2. Determine the coefficient of \( x^4 \) in the Maclaurin series expansion of \( \cos(x) \).

Maclaurin series for \( \cos(x) \):

\[ \cos(x) = 1 - \frac{x^2}{2!} + \frac{x^4}{4!} - \frac{x^6}{6!} + \cdots \]

Coefficient of \( x^4 \):

\[ \frac{1}{4!} = \frac{1}{24} \]

Final Answer: \(\frac{1}{24}\)

3. Identify the critical points of \( f(x) = \frac{x^3}{3} - \frac{x^2}{2} \).

First derivative:

\[ f'(x) = x^2 - x \]

Set derivative equal to 0:

\[ x^2 - x = 0 \]

\[ x(x-1) = 0 \]

Thus, \( x = 0 \) and \( x = 1 \).

Critical points: \( x=0 \) and \( x=1 \).

4. Evaluate the limit: \( \lim_{x \to \infty} x^{1/x} \)

Take natural log both sides:

\[ y = x^{1/x} \Rightarrow \ln y = \frac{1}{x} \ln x \]

Now limit:

\[ \lim_{x \to \infty} \ln y = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\ln x}{x} = 0 \quad (\text{because } \ln x \text{ grows slower than } x) \]

Thus:

\[ \ln y = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad y = e^0 = 1 \]

Final Answer: \( \boxed{1} \)

7. Define the partial derivative of a function \( f(x,y) \) with respect to \( y \) at point \( (x_0, y_0) \)

Definition:

The partial derivative of \( f(x,y) \) with respect to \( y \) is:

\[ \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}(x_0, y_0) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x_0, y_0+h) - f(x_0, y_0)}{h} \]

It measures how \( f \) changes as only \( y \) changes (keeping \( x \) fixed).

8. Given \( f(x,y) = x\cos(y) + ye^x \), find \( \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y \partial x} \)

First, partial derivative with respect to \( x \):

\[ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = \cos(y) + y e^x \]

Now differentiate wrt \( y \):

\[ \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y \partial x} = -\sin(y) + e^x \]

9. Find value of \( c \) satisfying MVT for \( f(x) = x^3 - x^2 \) on \( (-1,2) \)

Mean Value Theorem:

\[ f'(c) = \frac{f(2) - f(-1)}{2-(-1)} \]

Find \( f(2) \) and \( f(-1) \):

\[ f(2) = 8 - 4 = 4, \quad f(-1) = -1 -1 = -2 \]

Thus:

\[ f'(c) = \frac{4-(-2)}{3} = \frac{6}{3} = 2 \]

Find \( f'(x) \):

\[ f'(x) = 3x^2 - 2x \]

Set:

\[ 3c^2 - 2c = 2 \quad \Rightarrow \quad 3c^2 - 2c - 2 = 0 \]

Use quadratic formula:

\[ c = \frac{-(-2) \pm \sqrt{(-2)^2 - 4(3)(-2)}}{2(3)} \]

\[ c = \frac{2 \pm \sqrt{4+24}}{6} = \frac{2 \pm \sqrt{28}}{6} \]

Simplify:

\[ c = \frac{2 \pm 2\sqrt{7}}{6} = \frac{1 \pm \sqrt{7}}{3} \]

10. Find critical points and intervals where \( f(x) = x^3 - 12x - 5 \) is increasing or decreasing

\[ f'(x) = 3x^2 - 12 \]

Step 2: Set derivative = 0:

\[ 3x^2 - 12 = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad x^2 = 4 \quad \Rightarrow \quad x = \pm 2 \]

Step 3: Test intervals:

  • Pick \( x = -3 \) in \( (-\infty, -2) \): \( f'(-3) = 3(9) - 12 = 27 - 12 = 15 \) > 0 â†’ Increasing
  • Pick \( x = 0 \) in \( (-2, 2) \): \( f'(0) = -12 \) < 0 â†’ Decreasing
  • Pick \( x = 3 \) in \( (2, \infty) \): \( f'(3) = 3(9) - 12 = 27 - 12 = 15 \) > 0 â†’ Increasing

Critical points: \( x = -2, 2 \)

Increasing: \( (-\infty, -2) \cup (2, \infty) \)

Decreasing: \( (-2, 2) \)

13. Prove: \( (n-1)(I_n + I_{n-2}) = (\sqrt{3})^{n-1} \) where \( I_n = \int_0^{\pi/3} \tan^n(x) dx \)

Use Integration by Parts:

Idea:

\[ \tan^n(x) = \tan^{n-2}(x)(\sec^2(x)-1) \]

Expand and split:

\[ I_n = \int \tan^{n-2}(x) \sec^2(x) dx - \int \tan^{n-2}(x) dx \]

Substitution \( u = \tan(x) \), then \( du = \sec^2(x) dx \).

Thus:

\[ I_n = \frac{1}{n-1} \left( \tan^{n-1}(x) \right)_0^{\pi/3} - I_{n-2} \]

Simplify values of \( \tan(\pi/3) = \sqrt{3} \) to get the final result.
